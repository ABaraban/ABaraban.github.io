Choosing Between Roth and Traditional Retirement Accounts

June 2024

A common question for people saving for retirement is whether to contribute to a Roth IRA (post-tax) or a Traditional IRA (pre-tax). At its core, this choice is a bet on what your tax rate will be now vs. in retirement.

The Essential Tradeoff

With a Traditional IRA, you deduct your contribution from your income today, reducing current taxes. Your investments grow tax-deferred. When you withdraw, you pay income tax on both contributions and earnings.

With a Roth IRA, you contribute money you've already paid taxes on. Your investments grow tax-free, and qualified withdrawals are completely tax-free in retirement.

The Tax Equation

Suppose you can contribute $C$ dollars now. Let's compare what you'll have to spend in retirement from each account, ignoring investment limits:

  • Let $T_0$ = your marginal tax rate today
  • Let $T_1$ = your marginal tax rate at withdrawal (retirement)
  • Let $r$ = investment rate of return, $n$ = years until withdrawal

Traditional IRA:
You contribute pre-tax dollars. The account grows to $C(1 + r)^n$. On withdrawal, you pay taxes:

After-tax amount: \( C(1 + r)^n \times (1 - T_1) \)

Roth IRA:
You pay taxes upfront, so the initial contribution is smaller:

After-tax dollars to invest: \( C \times (1 - T_0) \)
Account grows to: \( C(1 - T_0) \times (1 + r)^n \)

Which is better?

The Roth and Traditional IRAs are equivalent if and only if your tax rate in retirement is the same as it is today ($T_0 = T_1$):

\( C(1 + r)^n (1 - T_1) = C(1 - T_0)(1 + r)^n \)
So, the only thing that matters is whether \(T_1\) is greater or less than \(T_0\).

  • If you expect your tax rate in retirement to be higher than today (\(T_1 > T_0\)), Roth is better.
  • If you expect your tax rate to drop in retirement (\(T_1 < T_0\)), Traditional is better.

Other Considerations

  • Required Minimum Distributions: Traditional accounts force you to withdraw at a certain age, Roth IRAs do not.
  • Contribution/income limits may affect your eligibility for either account type.
  • Roth IRAs offer flexibility (no tax on qualified withdrawals at any age), which can be beneficial if you need funds early.

Bottom Line

Most of the time, the Roth vs. Traditional choice simply comes down to asking: Do you expect to pay a higher or lower tax rate in retirement? If you expect higher taxes later, the Roth is more attractive. If you expect lower taxes, Traditional is generally better.
